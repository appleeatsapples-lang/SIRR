Chapter 01 · علم الأسماء

Arabic abjad and the science of letters.

Lineage

The science of letters in Arabic tradition (علم الحروف) traces to the early Islamic centuries and was developed at length by Sufi scholars, most extensively by Ibn Arabi (d. 1240). Its computational system, حساب الجمل (abjad numerology), assigns numerical values to the 28 Arabic letters in their classical ordering: أبجد · هوز · حطي · كلمن · سعفص · قرشت · ثخذ · ضظغ. The tradition is treated as علم — a scholarly discipline — when kept within Quran and Sunnah, and is held distinct from التنجيم (predictive astrology), which falls outside its boundary.

Computation
letters · سارة خالد س = 60 · ا = 1 · ر = 200 · ة = 5   →  266
خ = 600 · ا = 1 · ل = 30 · د = 4   →  635

sum and reduce 266 + 635 = 901 → 9 + 0 + 1 = 10 → 1 + 0 = 1
1
Reading

The reduced abjad sum of 1 is الواحد — unity. In abjad logic, 1 corresponds to alif (ا), the first letter of the alphabet, the upright stroke that begins every act of writing. Names that reduce to 1 in this tradition are read as oriented toward singularity of purpose — not isolation, but a structural inclination to lead from oneself rather than to be carried by group consensus. The reading describes a tendency in the name's structure, not a destiny.

What this tradition cannot tell you

Abjad reads the structure of a name. It does not predict events. It does not name compatibilities, marriages, or futures. A practitioner working within tradition will tell you what your letters incline toward — never what will happen.

Chapter 02 · Πυθαγόρειος

Pythagorean numerology, a Western convention.

Lineage

Modern Western numerology calls itself Pythagorean by tradition, though the actual letter-to-number system in use today was popularized in the late 19th and 20th centuries — primarily by L. Dow Balliett (The Day of Wisdom According to Number Vibration, 1903) and Florence Campbell (Your Days Are Numbered, 1931). The historical Pythagoras (d. ~495 BCE) taught that "all is number" but did not leave a letter-mapping system. The contemporary Pythagorean system maps Latin letters to digits 1–9 cyclically and reduces sums to a single digit.

Computation
letter values · A=1, B=2, …, I=9, J=1, … S=1 · A=1 · R=9 · A=1   →  12
K=2 · H=8 · A=1 · L=3 · I=9 · D=4   →  27

sum and reduce 12 + 27 = 39 → 3 + 9 = 12 → 1 + 2 = 3
3
Reading

A reduced value of 3 in the Pythagorean tradition is read as expressive — communicative, creative, social. It is the number of the artist and the speaker; the number of voice. The reading carries a caution as well as a strength: the same expressiveness that opens doors can dilute focus when scattered across too many channels at once.

What this tradition cannot tell you

Pythagorean numerology computes on Latin-letter spellings. It has no independent claim on the structure of an Arabic name; it reads the spelling, not the name. Where the name has multiple Latin transliterations (Sara / Sarah / Saara), the system produces multiple results — each correct for the spelling fed in, none of them canonical.

Chapter 03 · Chaldean

Chaldean numerology, a different convention on the same name.

Lineage

The Chaldean system takes its name from the Chaldeans, a Semitic people of southern Babylonia (c. 9th–6th century BCE), though direct continuity with that civilization is not historically established. The contemporary Chaldean letter-mapping system was popularized by 20th-century occultists working in the Cheiro and Sepharial lineage. It differs structurally from Pythagorean: it assigns digits 1–8 only (not 1–9), treating 9 as sacred and reserved.

Computation
letter values · Chaldean assigns 1–8, no 9 S=3 · A=1 · R=2 · A=1   →  7
K=2 · H=5 · A=1 · L=3 · I=1 · D=4   →  16

sum and reduce 7 + 16 = 23 → 2 + 3 = 5
5
Reading

A reduced value of 5 in the Chaldean tradition is read as movement-oriented — change, travel, restlessness, freedom-seeking. The Chaldean 5 carries a sharper edge than the Pythagorean 5: it is unsettled rather than expansive. Where Pythagorean reads its own 5 as adventurous, Chaldean reads its 5 as the principle of change itself, sometimes resisted, sometimes embraced.

Where this chapter and the previous one differ

The same name produces 3 in Pythagorean and 5 in Chaldean. SIRR does not pick. Both are shown because picking one would suppress the structural fact that letter-to-number numerology in the Latin alphabet has no canonical answer — it has competing conventions. The divergence itself is part of what the engine reports.

Chapter 04 · Hellenistic

Hellenistic astrology, computed on time and place.

Lineage

Hellenistic astrology emerged in late 2nd-century BCE Egypt, fusing Babylonian observational astronomy, Egyptian decanic systems, and Greek philosophical frameworks. Its principal sources are Vettius Valens (Anthology, 2nd c. CE), Dorotheus of Sidon (Carmen Astrologicum), and Ptolemy. The tradition was largely lost in the Latin West for nearly a millennium and has been recovered in scholarly form since the 1990s through translation projects led by Robert Schmidt, Robert Hand, and Chris Brennan.

Chart elements at this date and place
primary luminary Sun · 24° 17′ Pisces
dispositor: Jupiter (traditional ruler of Pisces)

sect day chart (born 14:30, after sunrise)
sect light: Sun · primary benefic in sect: Jupiter

moon Moon · 12° Scorpio (approximate)
♓
Reading

With the Sun in Pisces in a day chart, the sect light is in a sign ruled by Jupiter — a structurally favorable placement in Hellenistic logic. The tradition does not read this as "you will have good fortune." It reads it as "the chart's primary luminary is housed by a benefic ruler in the dominant sect, which is one configuration of structural support." The Moon in Scorpio brings depth and emotional intensity to a chart that might otherwise be read as diffuse; it functions as ballast.

What this chapter requires that we don't have

Hellenistic astrology depends on the Ascendant (rising sign) and house cusps, which require birth time accurate to within minutes. With approximate birth time, the chart can be sketched at the level of luminaries and sect, but house-based delineations — career, marriage, body — are speculative. SIRR will produce the full chart only with precise time; otherwise the chapter remains at the luminary level and is honest about it.

See also · Chapter 05 — Jyotiṣa, where the same Sun falls under Jupiter's rulership but in sidereal Mīna rather than tropical Pisces.

Chapter 05 · ज्योतिष

Jyotiṣa, the Vedic science of light.

Lineage

Vedic astrology (ज्योतिष, jyotiṣa, "science of light") is one of the six classical Vedāṅgas, traditionally rooted in the late Vedic period (c. 1500–500 BCE) and codified in Sanskrit texts including Parāśara's Bṛhat Parāśara Horā Śāstra and Varāhamihira's Bṛhat Saṃhitā (6th c. CE). The tradition uses the sidereal zodiac (nirayaṇa) — fixed against the actual stars — rather than the tropical (sāyana) zodiac used by Western and Hellenistic astrology. The standard correction between the two zodiacs (the ayanāṃśa) for 1990 is approximately 23°43′ on the Lahiri reckoning.

Chart elements at this date and place
sun · sidereal (Lahiri ayanāṃśa) tropical 24°17′ Pisces − 23°43′ ≈ 0°34′ Pisces (Mīna)
dispositor: Jupiter (Bṛhaspati)

moon · approximate sidereal ~18° Libra (Tulā)
nakshatra: Viśākhā (lord: Jupiter)

vimshottari dasha at birth Jupiter mahā-daśā (entered via Viśākhā nakshatra at birth)
मीन
Reading

In Jyotiṣa, with both Sun and Moon disposited by Jupiter (Bṛhaspati) — the Sun in Mīna which Jupiter rules, and the Moon in Viśākhā which Jupiter also lords — the chart's primary luminaries are bound to a single benefic disposition. The reading places this not as fortune but as a karmic structure: the chart's main themes will unfold under Jupiter's signature, which Vedic tradition associates with teaching, expansion, dharma, and the transmission of knowledge. The early Vimshottari dasha is also Jupiter, reinforcing the structural pattern.

Where this chapter and Hellenistic touched

Both chapters find the Sun under Jupiter's rulership — Hellenistic via tropical Pisces, Vedic via sidereal Mīna. The structural agreement is interesting because the two traditions disagree on the zodiac's relationship to the equinox. Same star-region, two different framings of why it matters. SIRR notes the agreement; it does not flatten the difference between sidereal and tropical into a single claim.

What this chapter requires that we don't have

Like Hellenistic, Jyotiṣa needs precise birth time for accurate house cusps, divisional charts (vargas), and full dasha computations. With approximate time, the chapter can offer luminaries and an approximate Moon nakshatra; the divisional charts, planetary friendships, and yoga combinations that constitute the heart of a full Jyotiṣa reading are reserved until precise time is provided.

Chapter 06 · 八字

BaZi, the four pillars of destiny.

Lineage

BaZi (八字, "Eight Characters") — also called Ziping (子平) after its Song-dynasty systematizer Xu Ziping (徐子平) — is the classical Chinese astrological system codified during the Tang and Song dynasties (7th–13th c. CE). It draws on the much older Heavenly Stems and Earthly Branches calendar system, the jiazi (甲子) cycle of 60 binomial pairings recorded in Shang oracle bone inscriptions (c. 1200 BCE). The system is structurally distinct from numerology and astrology in the Western sense: it does not produce a number or a chart of planets. It produces four binomial pillars — one each for Year, Month, Day, and Hour of birth.

Pillars at this date and place
year pillar · 1990 庚午 — Gēng Wǔ (yang Metal · Horse)

month pillar · after Jīngzhé solar term 己卯 — Jǐ Mǎo (yin Earth · Rabbit)

day pillar (requires ephemeris keyed to Chinese calendar; Day Master would be the Heavenly Stem of this pillar)

hour pillar · 14:30 local 未 — Wèi (Goat hour, 1–3 PM)
庚午
Reading

BaZi reads the chart as a balance of the five elements (五行, wǔxíng: wood, fire, earth, metal, water) as they appear across the four pillars. The Day Master — the Heavenly Stem of the Day pillar — represents the self, and its strength is read against the supporting and conflicting elements in the rest of the chart. With the Year pillar Geng-Wu, the year carries yang Metal energy on a Fire branch (Horse); the Month pillar Ji-Mao adds yin Earth on a Wood branch. Without the Day pillar, the structural reading sketches the framework but reserves the substantive elemental balance.

Why this chapter is structurally different from the others

BaZi does not reduce to a number. It does not produce a degree-position. Its output is a set of four binomial pairs and a structural elemental analysis. This means it cannot "agree" or "disagree" with the numerology chapters in the way Pythagorean and Chaldean disagreed with each other. The chapter stands on its own; the appendix at the end does not try to force a comparison.

What this chapter requires that we don't have

The Day Master computation requires an ephemeris keyed to the Chinese lunisolar calendar. Without it, the chapter shows three of the four pillars and the Hour branch, but the substantive Day Master reading and the ten-year luck pillars (大運, dàyùn) that follow from it are reserved until a precise computation is run.

Chapter 07 · גימטריה

Hebrew gematria — where the engine flags itself.

Note: The name "Sara Khalid" is Arabic, not Hebrew. To compute Hebrew gematria for an Arabic name, SIRR maps Arabic letters to their Hebrew cognates (ס↔س, א↔ا, ר↔ر, …) and applies Hebrew gematria values to the cognates. The result will track the Arabic abjad result by construction, because the cognate mapping preserves the value structure. A non-trivial agreement here is identity-by-construction, not cross-tradition convergence. SIRR flags this on every cognate-mapped result.
Lineage

Gematria is the Hebrew letter-value tradition recorded in rabbinic and Kabbalistic literature. Its values follow the Hebrew alphabet ordering: א=1, ב=2, … י=10, כ=20, … ק=100, ר=200, ש=300, ת=400. Major sources include the Sefer Yetzirah and the Zohar (13th c.). Within Jewish tradition, gematria is studied as exegesis — a hermeneutic for reading Torah and Talmud — not as a fortune-telling system.

Computation (cognate-mapped, with caveat)
cognate-mapped from Arabic abjad The Arabic letters of سارة خالد map to Hebrew cognates with the same value structure.
The reduced result is 1 — identical to the abjad chapter by construction.
1cognate
Reading

For a native Hebrew name, gematria of 1 would carry a substantive structural reading — oriented toward unity, the alef principle, the singular. For the cognate-mapped Arabic name "Sara Khalid," SIRR does not write a separate Hebrew reading. The result is identical to the Arabic abjad result by construction, and presenting it as independent would mislead. The Hebrew chapter exists in the report as a placeholder, with the caveat visible.

Why we still include this chapter

Two reasons. For names that are natively Hebrew, gematria is independent and substantive — and we want the chapter structure to be there when it applies. And for cognate-mapped cases, the visible caveat is itself the point: a cross-tradition engine that hides identity-by-construction behind a fake convergence claim is a worse engine than one that says, plainly, "this result is not independent of the previous chapter."

Appendix · cross-chapter notes

  • Disagreed Pythagorean (3) and Chaldean (5) on the same name. SIRR does not pick. The divergence itself is the finding: Latin-letter numerology has competing conventions, not a canonical answer.

  • Held its weight Among the chapters, abjad carries the most authority for this name because the name is native to that tradition. SIRR weights tradition-native computations more heavily than cognate-mapped ones when summarizing across chapters.
What SIRR does and doesn't do

Limits, stated upfront.

The depth of this work depends on being honest about what it isn't. We name the limits clearly so you can decide whether SIRR is for you.

  • Tell you who you'll marry, what year you'll get rich, or when you'll die.
  • Replace scholarly tradition or a teacher who has spent a lifetime in one of these systems.
  • Use AI to generate interpretations. Every claim comes from a deterministic rule set with visible derivation.

SIRR does

  • Compute each tradition on its own logic — Pythagorean stays Pythagorean, abjad stays abjad.
  • Tell you exactly where the traditions agree, where they disagree, and where they fall silent.
  • Show the derivation of every claim. Click and see the math.
  • Mark which results are tradition-native and which are cognate-mapped (and therefore identity-by-construction).
  • Run on a deterministic engine. Same inputs, same outputs, every time.
A note on Islamic orthodoxy. SIRR works within the boundary recognized by classical Islamic scholarship: علم الحروف and علم الأسماء are treated as scholarly disciplines (علم), with explicit avoidance of التنجيم — predictive horoscope claims that scholarly consensus considers prohibited. SIRR's outputs are structural, not predictive. If you are seeking fortune-telling, this is not the product for you.
